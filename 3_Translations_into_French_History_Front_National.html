Exercise 1: The Early Years of the FN

English Text (110 words):

The National Front was created in 1972 by a group of far-right movements. Jean-Marie Le Pen became its leader. At first, the party had little success. In the 1974 presidential elections, Le Pen only obtained 0.74% of votes. The party struggled for ten years before making its breakthrough in the 1983 local elections. The FN used immigration as a political weapon to attract voters. Many French people began to understand the party's ideas and identified with them more easily. For the FN, immigrants represented a threat to the French way of life. The party defended national independence and traditional values while rejecting European integration.

Exercise 2: Marine Le Pen's Strategy Change

English Text (110 words):

In 1998, Marine Le Pen encouraged her father to change the party's themes to attract a larger electorate. The FN began to harshly criticise poor working conditions and unemployment, blaming immigration and the European Union for everything. This strategy worked well. In April 2002, Jean-Marie Le Pen qualified for the second round of the presidential election but did not win. More and more voters embraced the anti-immigration ideas, though many were protest votes. In 2011, Marine succeeded her father at the head of the party. She started a change of strategy to make the party more acceptable to mainstream voters while keeping its core nationalist message.

Exercise 3: The FN's Electoral Success

English Text (110 words):

During the 1990s, the National Front attracted more and more voters. Bruno Mégret became the party's number two for ten years. In 1998, after a violent disagreement about Le Pen's provocative statements, Mégret left the party. The same year, Le Pen gave more power to his daughter Marine. The strategy change paid off. Anti-immigration ideas appealed to increasingly worried voters. Many felt that immigrants were taking their jobs and benefits. The party also benefited from protest votes against traditional parties. In 2014, the FN came first in the European elections with twenty-five percent of votes, proving that the extreme right had become a major political force in France.
