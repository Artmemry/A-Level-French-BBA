📌 Stimulus 1 — Le sabotage et les personnages clés

A · Les actes de sabotage des résistants français pendant la guerre ont été soutenus par la population.

  • Si les saboteurs auraient pu réussir sans le soutien de la population.
  • Comment les femmes ont contribué à la réussite des actes de sabotage.
❓ Q1 : Est-ce que les actes de sabotage auraient réussi sans le soutien de la population ?
Contenu indicatif
  • Popular support was structurally indispensable. Sabotage operations depended on a web of civilian contributions — passing messages, concealing equipment, creating diversions at checkpoints. Without this the exposure risk for active resisters would have been prohibitive.
  • Local knowledge was a strategic asset. Civilians planned escape routes, identified safe houses and warned of German movements — intelligence that could not have been improvised by outsiders.
  • Support was not unconditional. The ever-present threat of German collective reprisals meant that civilians constantly weighed the personal cost. Where reprisals were severe — as at Oradour-sur-Glane — local opinion could turn against the Resistance, illustrating the limits of popular support.
❓ Q2 : Comment est-ce que les femmes ont aidé les saboteurs ?
Contenu indicatif
  • A practical advantage at checkpoints. Women of all ages were far less likely to be stopped and searched by German soldiers than men of fighting age. This made them highly effective at transporting weapons, explosives and documents — the logistical lifeblood of sabotage networks.
  • Cover and concealment. Women provided civilian clothes and forged papers for Allied airmen who had parachuted into France, enabling them to move freely. They also acted as guides along escape routes. Jeanne Bohec's sabotage operations in Brittany — earning her the nickname la plastiqueuse à bicyclette — are a striking example of direct female involvement.
  • Broaden carefully. Once you have addressed the specific sabotage angle, develop your answer to cover women's wider roles — liaison work, clandestine press, shelter networks — as evidence of a contribution that went well beyond support functions.

B · Le succès de la Résistance a surtout dépendu de quelques personnages clés.

  • Le rôle important de certains personnages clés.
  • La contribution des journaux clandestins au succès de la Résistance.
❓ Q1 : Que pensez-vous de l'action menée par certains personnages clés ?
Contenu indicatif
  • De Gaulle provided legitimacy and external resources. His broadcast from London on 18 June 1940 gave scattered resisters a symbolic rallying point and ensured that the Free French could operate with Allied recognition and material support — something no internal group could have secured alone.
  • Jean Moulin's achievement was structural and irreplaceable. Parachuted back into France in 1942, his mission was to unify the fragmented resistance movements. By May 1943 he had created the Conseil National de la Résistance (CNR) — a unified council incorporating all major movements including the Communists, alongside political parties and trade unions. His arrest and death under Gestapo torture in June 1943 underlines both his centrality and the brutality of the occupation.
  • Challenge the stimulus. While key figures were essential, the Resistance ultimately depended on thousands of anonymous participants. The risk is overstating individual agency at the expense of the broader social mobilisation that sustained the movement.
❓ Q2 : Comment les journaux clandestins ont-ils aidé la Résistance ?
Contenu indicatif
  • Countering German-controlled information. The occupied press suppressed all news of Allied advances and resistance activity. Underground newspapers — produced by the Socialist Party, Emmanuel d'Astier and others — gave readers a counter-narrative, keeping alive awareness that the occupation could end and that resistance was widespread.
  • Recruitment and morale. The clandestine press was also an organisational tool: it recruited new members, co-ordinated action and sustained morale across regions. Distribution networks were themselves a form of active resistance.
  • Women's central role. Women were disproportionately involved in printing and distributing clandestine materials, exploiting the relative freedom of movement that German checkpoints afforded them compared to men.
📌 Stimulus 2 — Unité et souffrance

A · La Résistance n'était pas une force unifiée.

  • Votre opinion sur la déclaration.
  • Le rôle de Jean Moulin.
❓ Q1 : Est-il vrai de dire qu'il n'y avait pas une Résistance unifiée ?
Contenu indicatif
  • Initially, the statement is accurate. Resistance groups emerged independently, each driven by its own political ideology — Communist, Socialist, Christian, Jewish, trade unionist — with no shared command and little co-ordination.
  • Personal rivalries compounded the structural problem. Henri Frenay (Combat) and Emmanuel d'Astier (Libération) had a bitter personal and ideological rivalry that actively obstructed collaboration — illustrating how fragmented the movement was at its origins.
  • A structural divide also existed between the Free French forces around De Gaulle in London and the clandestine movements inside occupied France, with different resources, different risks and sometimes conflicting strategic priorities.
  • The statement needs nuancing after 1943. Moulin's creation of the CNR transformed the picture: the Resistance became, if not fully unified, at least co-ordinated enough to act as a coherent force in the final phase of the occupation.
❓ Q2 : Quel a été le rôle de Jean Moulin ?
Contenu indicatif
  • A mission of unification. Parachuted back into France in January 1942 as De Gaulle's personal representative, Moulin's task was to persuade competing resistance groups — with deep ideological differences — to accept a shared command structure and recognise De Gaulle's authority.
  • The Conseil National de la Résistance (CNR). In May 1943 he created the CNR, bringing together all major resistance movements — including the French Communist Party, which had previously held back — alongside political parties and trade unions. This was a remarkable achievement given the scale of the divisions involved.
  • His death and legacy. Arrested by the Gestapo and tortured to death in June 1943, Moulin became the symbolic martyr of the Resistance. The CNR survived him and gave the Resistance the political coherence it needed to play a role in post-liberation France.

B · La population française a beaucoup souffert à cause des actions des résistants pendant l'Occupation.

  • La part de responsabilité des résistants dans la souffrance de la population.
  • Le soutien de la population française envers la Résistance.
❓ Q1 : Comment les actions de la Résistance ont-elles contribué à la souffrance de la population ?
Contenu indicatif
  • German reprisals were systematic and deliberately brutal. Acts of sabotage or assassination triggered collective punishment: executions of hostages, mass deportations, confiscation of property. The logic was to make the civilian cost of sheltering the Resistance unbearable.
  • Oradour-sur-Glane as a defining case. On 10 June 1944, an SS unit massacred 642 men, women and children in reprisal for resistance activity in the region. The village was never rebuilt and stands as a memorial today. This shows how the violence of occupation, triggered in part by resistance activity, fell disproportionately on those with no direct involvement.
  • A genuine moral complexity. This does not mean the Resistance was wrong to act — but it raises a legitimate question about who bore the cost of resistance, and whether that suffering should be attributed to the resisters or to the occupiers who chose to respond with terror against civilians.
❓ Q2 : Comment la population a-t-elle aidé la Résistance ?
Contenu indicatif
  • Active engagement was a minority activity. Historians estimate only 1.5–3% of the French population were actively involved in organised resistance in 1942. But measuring support by formal membership alone is misleading.
  • Informal and passive support was far more widespread. Hiding a resistance fighter, providing food, running an errand, passing on overheard information — these acts required real courage even without armed combat. Collectively they sustained the Resistance's operational capacity.
  • Workplace resistance. Workers in German-controlled factories could slow production, sabotage machinery or refuse overtime. This form of everyday resistance was low-profile but significant in aggregate, particularly in industries vital to the German war effort.
📌 Stimulus 3 — Soutien populaire et efficacité

A · Les actions de la Résistance ne plaisaient pas à tous les Français.

❓ Q1 : Jusqu'à quel point est-ce que tous les Français approuvaient les activités de la Résistance ?
Contenu indicatif
  • Active support was limited but meaningful. With at most 3% directly engaged, the majority of French people did not participate — though this figure tells us little about the much larger proportion who offered informal support or quiet sympathy.
  • Significant sections were hostile or indifferent. Those who supported the Vichy regime, hoped for a German victory, or simply prioritised family safety had every reason to distance themselves. French collaborators — including members of the Vichy Milice paramilitary police — actively informed on resistance members.
  • Reprisals created a local backlash. When collective punishment fell on villages that had sheltered resisters, opinion could turn sharply against the Resistance — particularly where those killed had no connection to resistance activity.
  • Unarmed resistance commanded broader sympathy. Producing false papers, organising strikes, distributing underground newspapers — these forms of civil disobedience engaged a far wider cross-section of society and were more broadly approved than armed operations.
❓ Q2 : Quelles actions la Résistance a-t-elle organisées pendant l'Occupation ?
Contenu indicatif
  • Armed and military operations. Targeted assassinations, sabotage of railways, bridges and factories, guerrilla attacks on German troop movements — particularly significant in the weeks around the Normandy landings.
  • Civil and clandestine operations. Production of underground newspapers, forging of identity documents, organising strikes and demonstrations, creating escape networks for Allied prisoners of war and airmen.
  • The two dimensions were complementary. Armed operations demonstrated that France had not surrendered its dignity; civil operations built the organisational infrastructure and popular legitimacy on which the armed Resistance depended.

B · Sans Jean Moulin, la Résistance n'aurait pas été si efficace.

❓ Q1 : Pourquoi la Résistance était-elle dans un si mauvais état avant l'arrivée de Jean Moulin ?
Contenu indicatif
  • Deep political fragmentation. Each movement had its own political culture and objectives. The French Communist Party (PCF) is a key case: because of the Nazi-Soviet non-aggression pact signed in 1939, Stalin directed European Communist parties not to resist the German occupation. The PCF therefore held back in the early years, causing a damaging split on the French left.
  • Lack of co-ordination wasted effort. Without a shared command structure, operations were sporadic and uncoordinated. Groups duplicated work, competed for resources and — at worst — treated each other with suspicion. The collective impact was far less than the sum of its parts.
  • Personal conflicts among leaders. The rivalry between figures such as Frenay and d'Astier was not merely ideological but personal, and it actively impeded the collaboration that effective resistance required.
❓ Q2 : Comment la Résistance a-t-elle contribué à la victoire des Alliés ?
Contenu indicatif
  • Moulin's unifying legacy. The CNR (May 1943) gave the Resistance political coherence it had previously lacked. For the first time all major movements — including the PCF, which joined the anti-German struggle after Germany invaded the Soviet Union in June 1941 — operated under a shared framework.
  • A real but limited military contribution. The Resistance's military contribution to Allied victory was significant in specific operations — notably in slowing German reinforcements after D-Day (for example through Opération Tortue) — but historians generally conclude it was not decisive in purely military terms.
  • Intelligence, morale and political legacy. Communication networks provided vital pre-D-Day intelligence. Perhaps equally important was the symbolic dimension: the Resistance kept alive French agency and national dignity during four years of occupation, and gave post-war France a political identity — and a myth of collective heroism — it could not otherwise have claimed.
📌 Stimulus 4 — Le Parti communiste français (PCF)

B · Le Parti communiste français n'a pas joué un grand rôle dans la Résistance.

❓ Q1 : Quelle attitude le PCF avait-il envers la Résistance au début de la guerre ?
Contenu indicatif
  • The Nazi-Soviet Pact placed the PCF in an impossible position. After Germany and the Soviet Union signed their non-aggression pact in August 1939, Stalin directed Communist parties across Europe to avoid provoking Germany. The PCF adopted an ambivalent stance and initially refused to support armed resistance against either the Germans or Vichy.
  • This created a damaging internal split. Many rank-and-file Communists found this position untenable and left to join resistance groups independently — a fracture between official party policy and the instincts of individual members.
  • A serious blow to the party's credibility. At a moment of national occupation, the largest party of the French left was urging restraint. This fundamentally compromised both its moral authority and its claim to represent the French working class.
❓ Q2 : Pourquoi le PCF a-t-il changé de politique plus tard ?
Contenu indicatif
  • Germany's invasion of the Soviet Union transformed everything. On 22 June 1941, Hitler launched Operation Barbarossa against the USSR, breaking the pact. Stalin immediately reversed course, calling on Communists everywhere to fight fascism. The PCF threw itself into the Resistance with remarkable energy, rapidly building some of the most effective clandestine networks and armed groups.
  • The PCF became a major force in the unified Resistance, joining the CNR in 1943 and contributing significantly to operations in the final phase of the occupation — particularly in urban areas and among industrial workers.
  • Post-war mythology and historical revision. After liberation, the PCF claimed the title of parti des 75 000 fusillés — presenting itself as the backbone of the Resistance. Historians have established that the real figure of all Resistants executed was around 4,500, amongst them Communist Party members, and that the claim was a post-war electoral strategy rather far from historical accuracy. The episode illustrates how the memory of the Resistance was actively manipulated post war.
